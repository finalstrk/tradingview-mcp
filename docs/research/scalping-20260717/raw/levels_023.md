# Research Report: Round-Number Price Levels in FX Intraday

**topic_id:** levels_023  
**family:** levels  
**date:** 2026-07-17  
**question:** Round-number price levels in FX intraday: academic and practitioner evidence

## Analysis

Bottom line: round-number order clustering and short-horizon price reactions are well supported; profitable retail scalping is not. I found no round-number FX study or GitHub repository combining reproducible code/data, realistic costs, and genuine out-of-sample results.

1. **Round-number fade (research-derived).** Markets: USD/DEM, USD/JPY, GBP/USD; one-minute Reuters quotes, 1996–98. Rule: when price approaches within 0.01% of a level ending 00 or 50, trade against the approach; evaluate/exit after 15 minutes. No stop was tested. Filter: 09:00–16:00 New York. Reversal rates were 59.3%, 60.1%, and 58.1%, versus 54.8%, 57.3%, and 56.6% at random levels; 58/57/58 ten-day blocks, with significance for DEM and JPY but not GBP. No PF, P&L, costs, trade count, or OOS. [Peer-reviewed study](https://ideas.repec.org/a/eee/jimfin/v24y2005i2p219-241.html); [full methodology/tables](https://www.newyorkfed.org/medialibrary/media/research/staff_reports/sr150.pdf). **Grade A for the phenomenon, not profitability.** Pine v6: **partial**—logic is programmable, but TradingView bars lack the study's bid/ask quote path. The 2.8–4.5-point hit-rate uplift may vanish with asymmetric payoff, spread, and slippage; cost survival is unproven.

2. **Post-round-number continuation ("stop cascade").** Same markets/data/session. Research proxy: enter with the move after crossing an 00/50 level; exit 15 minutes later. No stop. Mean continuation after round crossings versus arbitrary crossings was 0.0608% vs 0.0537% (DEM), 0.0689% vs 0.0598% (JPY), and 0.0492% vs 0.0459% (GBP); round levels outperformed in 51/58, 33/57, and 36/58 blocks. [Fed PDF](https://www.newyorkfed.org/medialibrary/media/research/staff_reports/sr150.pdf). **Grade A for microstructure.** Pine: **partial/no for reproducing the claim** because the paper classifies a "crossing" using where price is 15 minutes later—look-ahead if treated as an entry signal. A causal bar-cross rule is implementable but untested. The incremental advantage is only about 0.3–0.9 pip-equivalent, so realistic retail costs probably consume it.

3. **Kathy Lien "Fading the Double Zeros."** Markets: general FX, examples USD/JPY, GBP/USD, USD/CAD; 15-minute. Long when price is below its 20-SMA and descending toward a double zero: buy 10–15 pips above it; reverse for shorts when above the SMA. Stop 20 pips beyond the level, making approximately 30–35 pips risk. Close half at 1R, move remainder to breakeven, then trail by a two-bar extreme or MA-based stop. Use quiet/no-major-news conditions and narrow-range pairs. Reported evidence is only selected examples: +35/+35, +35/+135, and +35/+40 pips; no denominator, losses, PF, costs, or OOS. [Rules and examples](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1002/9781119212997.ch10). **Grade C.** Pine: **partial**—rules are codable, but news exclusion, "significant confluence," and alternative trailing methods are discretionary. Costs are small relative to 30–35-pip risk, but there is no credible evidence of positive expectancy.

4. **USD/JPY half-yen touch fade.** One-minute MT5 logic: fade each ¥0.50 level exactly on touch; apparent final TP/SL 1 pip, one position at a time, no session filter. Report: PF≈1.22 over 42,479 trades, but no period, win rate, cost assumptions, data, or OOS; the author later discovered the test was accidentally long-only after tuning parameters on the same sample. [Code and report](https://note.com/deft_jaguar2334/n/n77e34ca5a8eb?hl=en). **Grade B methodology, very weak evidence.** Pine: **partial**—one-pip TP/SL requires tick sequencing unavailable reliably on one-minute OHLC. Spread/slippage almost certainly erase PF 1.22.

Survivorship bias is limited in major FX pairs, but pair/session/broker-result selection remains. Overfitting risk is modest in the academic predefined tests but severe in the practitioner rules. Overall evidence supports "round numbers affect order flow," not "retail round-number scalping is profitable."
