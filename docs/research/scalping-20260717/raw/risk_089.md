# Risk_089 Research Report

**topic_id:** risk_089  
**family:** risk  
**date:** 2026-07-17  
**question:** Tilt-control / kill-switch rules: any measured evidence they improve outcomes?

## Analysis

The evidence is weak and mixed. I found no Grade-A randomized or reproducible study showing that a fixed daily-loss or consecutive-loss switch improves net intraday returns. The strongest literature measures loss-induced behaviour, not the switch's counterfactual performance.

1. **Morning-loss / afternoon-off switch (inferred, not directly tested).** CBOT T-Bond futures; transaction-level intraday data, 1998. Proposed rule: trade normally before midday; if cumulative morning P&L < 0, flatten at midday and take no afternoon entries; reset next session. Coval–Shumway studied >5 million transactions and 1,082 traders: morning losers had a 31.2% probability of above-average afternoon risk versus 27.0% for winners, were approximately 15% more likely to initiate price-setting trades, and those prices reversed 27% more over the next five minutes. Win rate, PF, switch-on/off return, costs and OOS were not reported. [Journal paper/PDF](https://www.tylergshumway.org/Coval-BehavioralBiasesAffect-2005.pdf). **Grade A for behavioural evidence, not for switch efficacy.** Pine v6: **yes**—track session P&L and disable entries after a specified midpoint. Survivorship is limited by comprehensive exchange data, but selecting traders with ≥1,500 trades and ≥100 active days favours survivors. A later peer-reviewed study using 1995 CME Deutsche-mark, Swiss-franc, live-cattle and pork-belly trades found more afternoon effort/risk after losses but **no deterioration in risk-adjusted performance**; therefore stopping would potentially discard profitable activity. [Contradictory study](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1386418109000421). Retail spreads may make marginal "recovery" trades worse than floor-trader results, but that remains an inference.

2. **Daily equity-loss limiter.** Crude-oil futures, 120-minute bars, five years. Entry: with lookback 10, buy next bar when close crosses above close[10] while crossing below close[5]; reverse conditions for short. Exit: 2×ATR(14) stop, capped at $3,000/contract. Kill rule: when open plus closed P&L since midnight falls below −$2,500, flatten next bar and block entries until the next day. No session filter. Baseline: $40,030 net, 92 trades, 280.7% return, −$14,260 drawdown; optimized limiter: $47,675, 95 trades, 344.7%, −$13,830 drawdown. WR/PF, commissions, slippage and OOS absent. Limits below roughly $2,000 worsened performance; above $4,000 never triggered. [Code and methodology](https://easylanguagemastery.com/building-strategies/algorithmic-trading-tip-building-risk-protection-into-your-trading/). **Grade B. Pine: yes**, although evidence does not transfer to 1–15-minute bars. Severe overfitting: $2,500 was selected after testing $500–$10,000 on one strategy. Single-market/strategy survivorship and omitted costs make survival of the apparent improvement doubtful.

3. **Post-loss cooling-off.** Same crude-oil strategy. After any losing exit, suppress otherwise-valid entries for N bars; ordinary ATR stop remains. At optimized N=10 bars: net fell from $40,030 to $39,445, trades 92→89, return 280.7%→256%, drawdown worsened from −$14,260 to −$15,400. No costs/OOS/WR/PF. [Source](https://easylanguagemastery.com/building-strategies/algorithmic-trading-tip-building-risk-protection-into-your-trading/). **Grade B; Pine: yes.** This is concrete negative evidence: mechanical cooling-off did not help.

4. **5-minute momentum with layered switches.** ES/NQ, 2011–2026. Entry: two-bar close beyond a 90-day percentile "noise area," volume above the rolling 20-bar median; 09:30–15:00 ET. Exit on re-entry after ≥3 bars, 78 bars maximum, or 16:00. Controls: daily loss `min(5% equity,$5,000)`; 3% five-minute move triggers flattening plus 30-minute halt; 20% portfolio drawdown stops indefinitely. Reported 3,847 trades, 54.3% WR, PF 1.64, 8.5% CAGR, Sharpe 1.18, OOS Sharpe 0.94; $4.20 commission plus one tick/side. [Report](https://misango.me/static/Papers/Intraday_Momentum_Paper/Intraday_Momentum_Research_Report.pdf). **Grade B, low confidence; Pine: partial** because portfolio equity/allocation and cross-symbol controls are awkward. No control ablation means these numbers do **not** prove the switches helped. Code/data are absent, the boundary formula appears internally ambiguous, and the report stress-tests the 2010 flash crash despite claiming a 2011 start. Extra 0.5-tick slippage reduced CAGR by 2.7 percentage points, so the edge is execution-sensitive.

Bottom line: kill switches are defensible as tail-loss, operational-error and behavioural controls—not as demonstrated alpha enhancers. Pre-register thresholds and compare no-switch versus switch on untouched OOS data with retail costs; otherwise apparent improvements are likely selection bias.
