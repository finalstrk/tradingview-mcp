# Research: Sample sizes needed for statistically meaningful win-rate/PF claims: quantitative guidance

**Topic ID:** meta_092
**Family:** meta
**Date:** 2026-07-17
**Question:** Sample sizes needed for statistically meaningful win-rate/PF claims: quantitative guidance

---

For independent trades, "100 trades is enough" is indefensible. Using binomial power calculations, detecting a true 55% win rate against 50% requires about 617 trades at one-sided α=5%, 80% power; 60% needs about 153. For estimation, 384/1,067/2,401 trades give approximately ±5/±3/±2 percentage-point 95% precision near 50%. At 55 wins per 100, the Wilson 95% CI is 45.2–64.4%; even 220/400 only yields 50.1–59.8%. These calculations follow standard proportion methodology from [NIST](https://www.itl.nist.gov/div898/handbook/prc/section2/prc242.htm). Clustered same-day/same-symbol trades reduce effective sample size; inference should block-bootstrap by day and market regime.

PF has no universal minimum because it is a ratio of random gross profits to gross losses and depends strongly on payoff skew/tails. Under the artificial 1:1 payoff case, PF 1.2 implies WR 54.55% and needs roughly 746 independent trades to distinguish PF>1 with α=5%, 80% power; PF 1.5 implies WR 60% and roughly 153. In real strategies, require an OOS day-block-bootstrap 95% lower PF bound above 1, positive expectancy, and multiple-testing correction. The [Deflated Sharpe Ratio](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=2460551) addresses selection bias, non-normality and repeated trials.

**SPY first/last-half-hour momentum.** Market/timeframe: SPY, 30-minute intervals. Rule: compute return from prior close through 10:00 ET; at 15:30 go long if positive, otherwise short; exit 16:00. No base filter; effect strengthens with volatility, volume and macro-news days. From 1993–2013—roughly 5,200 daily opportunities—the strategy reported 6.67% annual return, 6.19% volatility, Sharpe 1.08; OOS \(R^2\) 1.4%; WR, PF and exact trade count were not reported. Results reportedly survive "reasonable" costs, but accessible reporting lacks a precise retail cost schedule. Evidence **A**: peer-reviewed JFE [paper](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0304405X18301351). Pine v6: **yes**. Survivorship risk low for SPY, but asset-selection/publication and post-2013 decay remain. Tight SPY spreads make survival plausible, though unverified today.

**Stocks-in-Play 5-minute ORB.** US stocks; form 09:30–09:35 range. If candle closes above/below its open, place buy-stop at its high/sell-stop at its low; stop 10% of 14-day ATR; otherwise exit 16:00. Filters: price>$5, 14-day volume≥1m, ATR>$0.50, opening Relative Volume≥100%, top 20 Relative Volume. Reported 2016–2023, >7,000-stock universe: WR 48.4%, net return 1,637%, Sharpe 2.81, alpha 35.8%; **PF and trade count omitted**, no independent OOS. Commission $0.0035/share, but spread, slippage, locate/short constraints are absent. Evidence **B**: detailed SSRN [paper](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=4729284), no reproducible code/data. Pine: **partial**—single-symbol rule works, but point-in-time top-20 cross-sectional ranking does not. CRSP includes delisted stocks, reducing survivorship bias; same-sample discovery of the Relative-Volume filter creates severe overfitting risk. Retail cost survival is doubtful for volatile news stocks.

**MNQ 30-minute ORB—negative result.** Define 09:30–09:55 ET high/low; enter at next-bar open after breakout; reported variant exits 15 five-minute bars later, without a stop. On 447 long OOS trades: WR 55.5%, net +2.82 points/trade, \(t=1.50\); short side N=428, WR 47.7%, −2.16 points. PF unreported. Expanding-window walk-forward, 2021–2025, with two-point round-trip friction; performance varied sharply by year and failed significance. Evidence **B**: detailed but non-peer-reviewed [preprint](https://doi.org/10.48550/arXiv.2605.04004). Pine: **yes**. No equity survivorship issue, but continuous-contract/roll choices and testing 14 signal families matter. Conclusion: the generic ORB edge does **not** reliably survive realistic retail costs.
