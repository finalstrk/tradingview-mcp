# research/scalping-20260717 :: risk_088

**topic_id**: risk_088  
**family**: risk  
**date**: 2026-07-17  
**question**: Position sizing for scalps (fixed fractional vs fixed risk): evidence-based guidance

---

Conclusion: There is no strong direct experiment proving fixed-fractional sizing superior to constant-dollar fixed risk for scalps. Sizing cannot create expectancy. For live trading, prefer stop-normalized fixed fractional—e.g., quantity = min(equity × 0.25–0.50% / stop distance, exposure cap)—because it compounds and automatically reduces dollars at risk after losses. For strategy research, first use constant dollars or 1R to prevent compounding from disguising a weak edge. Risk 1% only after genuine out-of-sample validation; cap correlated portfolio heat. Volatility targeting generally reduces tails, but Sharpe improvement is market-dependent ([Harvey et al.](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=3175538)).

1. Timely Opening-Range Breakout (TORB) — Grade A. Markets: DJIA, S&P 500, Nasdaq-100, HSI and TAIEX futures; 1-minute bars. During the underlying cash-market session, record high/low from session open through a market-specific "probe time"; buy the first subsequent break above that high or short below the low; close at cash-session end; no stop. Best probe times: DJIA 4m, S&P/Nasdaq 1m, HSI 151m, TAIEX 37m. Samples: 1,381–3,099 trades during 2001/03–2013; annual net return 8.95–20.28%, with assumed 0.01% transaction cost; pre/post-2007 subperiods broadly positive, though DJIA pre-2007 and S&P post-2007 were insignificant. Win rate and PF not reported; subperiods are robustness checks, not true OOS. Pine v6: yes on 1-minute bars. Survivorship: negligible for continuous index futures. Overfitting: serious—probe time selected for best full-sample result without multiple-testing correction. Retail survival: plausible only for liquid contracts, but old data and absent explicit spread/slippage modelling make current survival unproven. [Paper](https://doi.org/10.1109/ACCESS.2019.2899177).

2. Five-minute ORB on "Stocks in Play" — Grade B. Market: all US equities, 2016–2023; 5-minute opening bar. Filters: price >$5, prior-14-day average volume >1m shares, ATR(14) >$0.50; opening-bar relative volume ≥100% of its prior-14-day average; select top 20. If first bar closes above open, buy its high breakout; if below, short its low; doji=no trade. Stop = 0.10×daily ATR; otherwise exit 16:00 ET. Reported sizing: 1% stop-risk with 4× leverage cap. CRSP universe includes delisted stocks, reducing survivorship bias. Metrics: 48.4% hit rate, 1,637% compounded return, 41.6% annualized, Sharpe 2.81, MDD 12%; $0.0035/share commission; PF and trade count not reported; no OOS. The unfiltered version returned only 29%, Sharpe 0.48. Pine: partial—single-symbol rules yes, but Pine cannot rank the whole US universe's relative volume dynamically. Overfitting/capacity/short-availability risks are high; omission of bid–ask spread and slippage makes the reported 0.08R average edge for RVOL>100% doubtful for retail execution. [Paper](https://alexandria.unisg.ch/server/api/core/bitstreams/3c2989c4-688d-4d78-8a71-f02690990d51/content).

3. SPY Noise-Area Momentum — Grade B. On SPY intraday bars, calculate time-of-day upper/lower bands from the prior 14 sessions' mean absolute move from the open, gap-adjusted using prior close. At HH:00/HH:30, go long above the upper band or short below the lower; exit/reverse when price crosses the current-side band or VWAP; flat at 16:00. Metrics, 2007–early 2024: 7,668 trades, 37% trade hit rate, $0.09/share average P&L, 380% return, Sharpe 1.24, MDD 12%; PF and true OOS absent. Volatility sizing to 2% daily target, capped at 4×, raised reported Sharpe to 1.33 and return to 1,985%, but also MDD to 25%. Costs assumed $0.0035 commission plus only $0.001 slippage/share—probably optimistic versus market-order spread capture. Pine: yes on 1–15m bars, with 30-minute decision gating. No survivorship issue; substantial same-sample refinement/overfitting risk. [Paper](https://concretumgroup.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/Beat-the-Market.pdf).

Overall evidence is mixed: TORB has peer-reviewed historical support, but the two spectacular modern results lack clean OOS tests. Treat constant-dollar sizing as the diagnostic baseline and conservative fixed fractional as risk control—not as evidence of an edge.
